Module 1 Comprehensive Learning Journal

Foundations of Instructional Design

Date: October 5, 2025

This module taught me that Instructional Design (ID) is much bigger than I first thought. I used to think ID was mainly about making training materials look nice, but now I know it's a deep, professional method for solving problems.

1. What This Means for Me and the World

a. For Me (As a Future ID and Learner)

The main takeaway is that my job isn't to create "fun" content; it's to solve a performance problem. The ID field has become focused on **business and corporate needs** (Raible, 2020). This means I need to stop thinking like a writer and start thinking like a consultant. My success depends on **collaboration** with experts and checking if the training actually helps the company (Martin & Ritzhaupt, 2020).

b. For the ID Field

The field gets its strength from being **systematic**. Using the **ADDIE framework** (Analysis, Design, Development, etc.) shows that ID uses scientific knowledge to design learning (Schott & Seel, 2015). This helps ID move beyond being just an "education" job and prove its value by delivering a good **Return on Investment (ROI)**.

c. For Society

ID is always changing because of big events. The first real push for systematic training came from the need for **quick, mass training during World War II** (Molenda, 2023). Today, new **technology and media** are constantly forcing ID to adapt to new platforms, like online learning and apps, keeping the discipline relevant.

2. Thinking About My Old Ideas

Before this class, I mostly believed ID was about being good at development software. This idea came from seeing many job ads that only listed technical tools.

My New View: I was wrong. The module showed me that the most important step is **Analysis**—figuring out why a performance problem exists before designing anything (Schott & Seel, 2015). Focusing only on software meant I was missing the most valuable part: **diagnostic work**. I need to let go of the idea that technical skills are everything and start valuing **critical thinking and communication** more.

3. How This Learning Changes Me

a. My Growth

I am moving from having a **"developer mindset"** to a **"consultant mindset."** I see myself as someone who solves organizational problems through structured learning, not just someone who designs slides.

b. Shift in Perspective

My measure of success has changed. I used to ask, "Did the learner like the course?" Now I know I must ask, "Did the learner's performance improve and meet the business goal?"

c. Changes in Behavior

I will change how I start a project. Instead of talking about content right away, I will now spend most of my time asking tough questions to identify the **performance gap** first, making sure I am not creating training when another solution (like better tools or motivation) is needed.

4. Evaluating My Work

I think my performance in this first module was good. I grasped the main ideas quickly and successfully showed how the history, theory, and professional skills all fit together in my concept map and reflection. My best decision was making sure I linked everything back to the business's need for **ROI** (Raible, 2020).

5. My Plan for Next Time

My main goal is to get better at **Performance Analysis**.

  • Action (Professional): I will practice describing training as a solution to a **performance gap** so I can talk the language of business leaders.
  • Action (Behavioral): I will work on my **listening skills** to get better at **collaborating** and interviewing Subject-Matter Experts (Martin & Ritzhaupt, 2020). This will help me find the real root of a performance problem.
